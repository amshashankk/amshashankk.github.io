Empowering high-street retailers with digital payments

Small independent retailers couldn't afford card machines and were losing customers to larger competitors who could. I designed Voilo's end-to-end merchant experience — QR-based payments, digital loyalty programs, and promotional tools — cutting transaction costs by 90% and giving independent shops the digital infrastructure to compete.

01 — Business Context

Independent retailers were losing ground. Not on product quality — on infrastructure.

Voilo was building a payments and loyalty platform specifically for high-street independent retailers: the barbershop, the corner grocery, the independent cafe. These businesses were competing against chains that offered card payments, loyalty programmes, and digital receipts as standard. Independent shops couldn't afford the card terminals, the MDR fees, or the POS integration costs that came with mainstream payment infrastructure.

The opportunity Voilo identified: if you could offer independent retailers a QR-based payment system that required zero hardware, near-zero transaction fees, and loyalty mechanics out of the box, you'd unlock a segment that had been structurally underserved by fintech. The market was large, the competition was thin, and the founding team had domain credibility from earlier B2B work.

I joined at the zero stage. There was a product vision and a founding team. There was no design — no flows, no screens, no component library. My job was to build the design foundation while the engineering team built the backend infrastructure in parallel.

The merchant pain wasn't just the transaction fee. It was the full picture: bank trips to deposit cash, theft risk, bookkeeping overhead, inability to run loyalty schemes without physical stamp cards, and the inability to send digital promotions. Each of these was a separately manageable problem for a large retailer with infrastructure. For a 2-person shop, the cumulative burden was a real drag on time and margin.

The consumer pain was simpler: "I walked past the shop because I only had card and didn't know if they took it."

03 — Constraints

Designing for merchants who had never used a digital payment tool before.

The constraints on this project were unusual — not the typical "limited engineering bandwidth" constraints, but human constraints rooted in the target user profile:

  • The target merchant was not a digital-native user. Many had never set up a digital payment system. The onboarding flow had to be operable without any prior context — no assumed familiarity with QR codes, account dashboards, or settlement flows.
  • Merchants operated in active shop environments. The app had to be usable one-handed, at glance speed, while a customer was waiting. No deep menus, no multi-step payment flows.
  • The consumer-side experience (the payer) was entirely out of Voilo's control — consumers paid via their existing UPI apps (GPay, PhonePe, Paytm). Our design had to work with UPI's flow, not replace it.
  • Trust was a hard constraint. Merchants were being asked to trust a new platform with their revenue. Every design decision had to communicate reliability — instant confirmations, clear settlement timelines, legible transaction history.
  • Zero hardware. The entire merchant experience had to work on the Android smartphone they already owned, with no additional device, terminal, or printer required.
04 — Research & The One Insight

Merchants didn't want a payments app. They wanted something that would bring customers back.

The founding team's assumption was that the core value proposition was cost savings on transactions. After [X] merchant interviews, I found a stronger driver: customer retention. Every merchant I spoke to described the same scenario — a regular customer hadn't been in for two weeks and they had no way to reach them, offer them something, or know why they'd stopped coming.

The insight reframed the product's hierarchy. Payments were the entry point — the thing that made the value proposition credible and drove onboarding. But the loyalty and promotional tools were the long-term retention hook for merchants. If we designed the app as "payments + optional loyalty add-on," we'd undersell its value. If we designed it as "the tool that helps your regulars stay regular, with payments built in," we'd have a story merchants could act on.

"Cash is a hassle. It's risky and fiddly. But what I really want is for my regulars to keep coming. I don't know how to reward them right now."

Independent barber, [City], interviewed during discovery

The reframe. The home screen of the merchant app led with customer activity (who came in today, new vs returning split, loyalty progress) rather than transaction totals. Payments were always one tap away — but the product told a retention story first.

05 — Explorations

Three structural approaches explored before settling on the product architecture.

These were concept-level explorations tested with [X] merchants before any visual design work began.

Direction A

Payments-only MVP — loyalty as v2

Ship the QR payment flow, settlement dashboard, and transaction history. Hold loyalty and promotional tools for v2 post-traction validation.

Killed because: In concept testing, merchants who saw the payments-only concept described it as "just another UPI QR generator." The loyalty layer was the differentiator that made Voilo worth switching to. Without it, there was no compelling reason not to use a free GPay Soundbox instead.
Direction B

Merchant dashboard web-first

Build a web-based merchant dashboard as the primary surface — richer analytics, better for merchant management — with a companion QR display app for in-store use.

Killed because: Target merchants did not have desktop access in-store. A web-first approach would have added adoption friction for the core use case (accepting a payment) without meaningfully improving it. Mobile-first wasn't a preference; it was the constraint of the environment.
Direction C — Chosen

Unified mobile app — payments + loyalty + promotions

Single Android app covering the full merchant workflow: QR payment acceptance, real-time settlement view, customer loyalty tracking (stamp + point system), and promotional push to regulars. Home screen led with customer activity, not transaction totals.

Chosen because: Best matched the insight that loyalty was the primary long-term hook. Single surface reduced cognitive load for non-digital-native merchants. Home screen framing made the product feel like a business tool, not just a payment terminal.
06 — The Decision

The simplest payment flow possible. Everything else could be discovered.

The payment acceptance flow was the non-negotiable critical path. I designed it to be completable in 2 taps from the home screen: open app, tap "Accept Payment," display QR, confirm. No amounts entered by the merchant — the customer's UPI app captured the amount. No manual reconciliation — transactions appeared in the ledger automatically, with instant audio + visual confirmation.

The loyalty mechanics were designed to be automatic rather than managed. Merchants didn't configure point rules or manage reward redemptions manually — the system applied a default programme (stamp card: 10 visits = 1 free item, configurable) and handled tracking silently. The merchant's only required action was to confirm a redemption when a customer presented their earned reward. This removed the management overhead that had killed loyalty scheme adoption in previous B2B fintech products I'd studied.

The trade-off I accepted explicitly: the automatic loyalty defaults meant some merchants got a programme that didn't perfectly fit their business. A barber and a coffee shop have different optimal reward structures. I chose adoption over precision for v1 — get merchants using loyalty at all, then introduce configuration once they'd seen the value. Forcing configuration upfront would have added onboarding friction that killed the habit before it formed.

09 — Reflection

What I'd do differently. What this project taught me about 0-to-1 work.

What I'd do differently. I'd invest more in the consumer-side experience earlier. The merchant app was well-designed, but the customer's experience — scanning the QR, paying, receiving a digital loyalty stamp — was handled by third-party UPI apps. We had no control over that interaction, and no touchpoint to reinforce Voilo's brand. A lightweight consumer-side experience (even a simple web page showing loyalty balance) would have given merchants a more complete story to tell customers and us a retention surface we currently lacked.

What I underestimated. Trust signalling for merchants at onboarding. Even after completing the 3-step signup, some merchants were hesitant to accept their first real transaction because the platform was new and unfamiliar. I should have designed a "practice transaction" flow — a zero-risk test payment between the merchant and a test customer account — to close the trust gap before real money changed hands.

What I'll carry forward. In 0-to-1 work, the job is to reduce the distance between "I understand what this does" and "I'm using it right now." Every extra step in that journey is a place the product can lose the user permanently. At Voilo, the 2-tap payment flow wasn't a UX preference — it was the business's entire go-to-market strategy. The design had to make the value immediately tangible or the product didn't get a second chance.
